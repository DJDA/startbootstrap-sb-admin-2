MIRRORINGS

Inferno 1

Purgatorio 1

Paradiso 1

IN MEDIAS RES























*JM, DM (II, 27)

The poem thrusts the reader in medias res: “In the middle of the journey of our life” [Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita: 1]. The midpoint turns out to be a dark place where emotional serenity is lost along with any clear view of the horizon from which directional bearings might be recovered.

With Dante and Virgil, the reader has arrived at the “second kingdom” [secondo regno: 4] which is the middle phase of the journey through the three realms of the Poem. The Italian noun for “middle” recurs in a prepositional phrase near the beginning of the canto: “the calm and peaceful aspect of intervening air, pure to the horizon” [sereno aspetto / del mezzo, puro infino al primo giro: 14-15]. Here it refers to air as the diaphanous medium through which light is instantly actualized according to the Aristotelian theory of vision. The transparency and serenity of this medium present a sharp contrast to the obscurity and emotional turbulence associated with Dante’s experience of the smarrimento.


LIMINAL LOCATION ON THE EARTH’S SURFACE: BETWEEN HEIGHTS AND DEPTHS







*DM (II, 33, 46), JM

Brought back to his senses, Dante wanders on a crooked route between the heights and the depths. This liminal zone of alternating experiences – turbulent anxiety followed by rest and relief followed by further anxiety – anticipates his precarious position and conflicted experience in the Inferno.

Like the deeply symbolic landscape of the smarrimento, the coastal zone of Purgatory is literally liminal: Virgil and Dante find themselves neither on the Mountain itself, which is yet to be glimpsed, nor away from its protective landmass surrounded by the deep.


LOST WAY









*DM (II, 32), RH (II, 21)

Upon finding himself in the dark wood, Dante realizes that “the straight way was lost” [la diritta via era smarrita: 3]. Casting himself in the role of the lost man, Dante has strayed both literally (into the wood) and spiritually (into error), having abandoned “the true way” [la verace via:12].

The lost man returns in metaphor: “like a man who returns to the lost way, and until he reaches it, feels he goes in vain” [com’ om che torna a la perduta strada, / che ’nfino ad essa li pare ire in vano:119-120]. In fact Dante is now on the right road.


DARK VALLEY








*JM

Dante wanders alone along a dark wooded “valley” to its fearful end [là dove terminava quella valle / che m’avea di paura il cor compunto: 14-15]. The commentary tradition has identified this topographical depression with the Valley of the Shadow of Death.

Dante and Virgil have together reached the consolingly hopeful end of their journey through “the infernal valley” [la valle inferna: 45].


ILLUMINATED MOUNTAIN

















*JM

At the end of the valley lies “the foot of a hill” [piè d’un colle: 13], its “shoulders already clothed in the rays of the sun” [le sue spalle / vestite già de’ raggi: 16-17]. Virgil later refers to it as a mountain: “the delightful mountain that is the origin and cause of all joy” [il dilettoso monte / ch’è principio e cagion di tutta gioia: 77-78]. The commentary tradition has identified this topological elevation as the Mount of Contemplation, foreshadowing the meditative heights and Edenic summit of Mount Purgatory.

Instead of a hill, the “cliffs” [grotte: 48] of an immense “mountain” [monte: 108] loom behind Dante and Virgil, even though their gaze is initially directed away from the heights towards the maritime horizon and the stars of the southern celestial hemisphere shining down on Mount Purgatory.


PLANET ASSOCIATED WITH DIVINE LOVE




























*DM (II, 33), RM

Swathing the hillside are “the rays of the planet that leads men straight on every path” [raggi del pianeta / che mena dritto altrui per ogne calle: 17-18]. This planet is the sun. Dante’s journey began at the morning hour when “the sun was mounting with those stars that were with it when God’s love first set those beautiful things in motion” [principio del mattino, / e ’l sol montava ’n sù con quelle stelle / ch’eran con lui quando l’amor divino / mosse di prima quelle cose belle: 37-40]. The she-wolf, as the beast of insatiable greed, is viciously opposed to the love that moves the sun and the other stars. Hence the she-wolf’s main allegorical action: to drive Dante down “to where the sun is silent” [là dove ’l sol tace: 60], which is to say, to the depths where the darkness of the valley totally obscures the sun’s eloquent testimony to the primal impetus of the Creator’s Love.

Dante observes how the “fair planet that comfortingly prompts us to love caused all the east to laugh” [Lo bel pianeto che d’amar conforta / faceva tutto rider l’orïente: 19-20]. This planet is Venus. By the end of the canto, he will see the sun rising [lo sol...che surge omai: 107] as a repeat-sign of the divine love that first set the heavenly bodies in motion. Reflecting the benevolent light of the stars, the face of Cato glows “with so much light that I saw him as if the sun had been before him” [sì la sua faccia di lume, / ch’i’ ’l vedea come ’l sol fosse davante: 38-39].


UPWARD GLANCE, WITH PLEASANT EMOTIONAL CONSEQUENCES













*DM (II, 33)

As soon as Dante “looks on high and sees” the sun [guardai in alto e vidi: 16], his fearful distress is temporarily calmed [Allor fu la paura un poco queta: 19]. This upward glance is Dante’s last full view of the sky (at the literal level) until the final line of the first cantica.

As soon as Dante beholds Venus smiling above him and the four mysterious stars glittering near the south celestial pole, his eyes are not only aesthetically “pleased” [a li occhi miei ricominciò diletto: 16] but also relieved to be far away from the dead air that had afflicted his “chest and eyes” in Hell [l’aura morta / che m’avea contristati li occhi e ’l petto: 17-18]. This upward glance heralds many such observations of the ever-changing sky throughout the second cantica.


DESERTED SLOPE










*DM (II, 33), JM

After resting a while, Dante makes his way “along the deserted slope” [per la piaggia diserta: 29] where his left foot is always lower than the right. This slope is a precarious “steep” [erta: 31] where he is eventually forced to slide, “fleeing” or “ruining down” to “a lower place” [rovinava in basso loco: 61].

In contrast to the ruinous slope of the smarrimento, the coastal region at the base of Purgatory “slopes...down to its low edges” [ché di qua dichina / questa pianura a’ suoi termini bassi: 113-114] with a gentle gradation. Its slope forms a “lonely plain” [solingo piano: 118] down to the “deserted shore” [lito deserto: 130].


SEASHORE


















*DM (II, 33), JM

The dangerous shore of the epic tradition is recalled in metaphor, when Dante, after a distressing night alone in the valley, compares himself to a recently shipwrecked man: “one who, with laboring breath, having come out from the deep onto the shore, turns and looks back at the perilous waters” [quei che con lena affannata, / uscito fuor del pelago a la riva, / si volge a l’acqua perigliosa e guata: 22-24]. Though the dark wooded “shore” of the Inferno seems at first deserted, Dante's solitude will soon be broken by the three beasts followed by the shade of the epic poet Virgil.

Dante and Virgil literally arrive at the seashore, but not in the usual epic way (i.e. as shipwrecks cast up on a beach from the stormy deep). They have arrived there via a subterranean passage from the infernal abyss. The “trembling of the sea” [il tremolar de la marina: 117] is perceived in the distance, but its ominous sound does not threaten them. The beach may be empty and lonely for now, but it will soon be crowded with passengers from the Angel Boat. This stretch of beach is grassy and refreshingly dewy because of a protective breeze.


MEMORY OF ULYSSES















*DM (II, 33), RM

Ulysses is the epic hero whose harrowing experience of being washed ashore immediately springs to mind when Dante compares himself to a recently shipwrecked man (22-24). With this comparison Dante begins to identify himself as the New Ulysses.

Ulysses’s account of having glimpsed Mount Purgatory just before being swallowed by the sea (Inf. 26.133-135) is clearly recalled when Dante contemplates the sea from the Purgatorial shore, “which never saw any man sail its waters who afterwards experienced return” [che mai non vide navicar sue acque / omo, che di tornar sia poscia esperto: 131-132]. In contrast to the Old Ulysses, the New Ulysses will escape drowning and experience a joyous return from the afterlife.


VEGETATION






*JM

The “dark wood...savage, dense and harsh” [selva oscura... selvaggia e aspra e forte: 2, 5] not only confuses but also deeply depresses the pilgrim at the deadening outset of his smarrimento.

Low “grass” [erbetta: 124] and humble “reeds” [giunchi: 102] not only instruct but also amaze the pilgrim at the miraculously regenerative conclusion of his brief detour to the shore.


PROGRESS INITIALLY BLOCKED








*DM (II, 33), JM

Many an obstacle “impedes” [’mpedisce: 96] Dante’s progress in this canto: e.g. the dense wood; the turmoil of his passions; the loss of daylight; the looming mountain; the slippery slope; the three beasts; and even, for a little while, the unexpected appearance of Virgil’s shade.

The ascent of Dante and Virgil through Purgatory is initially impeded by the forbidding steepness of the Mountain itself; the unfamiliarity of the terrain to both the guide and his follower; and by the unexpected appearance of Cato’s shade, commanding them to descend to the shore.



HOPE LOST AND REGAINED















*MS, JM

When his frustrating encounter with the leopard brings him to the brink of despair, Dante regains “hope for good” [a bene sperar: 41] at the break of day. Hope weakens again when the lion blocks his progress, and it is lost altogether when his spirits are weighed down with terror at the sight of the she-wolf. As he confesses: “I lost the hope of the heights” [io perdei la speranza de l’altezza: 54].

Where hope of the heights was lost on the literal level of the smarrimento, it is initially lost on the typological level at the outset of the second cantica: the Ovidian magpies “lost all hope of pardon” [disperar perdono: 12] when they heard the Muse’s lyre. Hope of the heights is permanently regained on the literal level when the pilgrim is reassured by Cato that the rising sun will show him “how to take the mountain by an easier ascent” [lo sol vi mosterrà, che surge omai, / prendere il monte a più lieve salita: 107-108].


SHADE OF AN ANCIENT ROMAN













*DM (II, 33), MS, JM

Dante encounters the shade of Virgil of Mantua, who tells part of his biography: “I was born sub Julio, though it was late, and lived at Rome, under good Augustus ” [Nacqui sub Iulio, ancor che fosse tardi, / e vissi a Roma sotto ’l buono Augusto: 70-71] referring to Julius Caesar, the proto-emperor of Rome and adoptive father of Octavian. As an apologist of the Empire, Virgil was a fervent admirer of Augustus.

Dante and Virgil encounter the shade of Cato of Utica, who tells part of his biography: “Marcia so pleased my eyes while I was back there...that whatever favor she sought from me, I granted” [Marzïa piacque tanto a li occhi miei / mentre ch’i’ fu’ di là...che quante grazie volse da me, fei: 85-87]. As a defender of the Republic, Cato was a fervent opponent of Julius Caesar and an enemy of the imperialist forces led by Octavian.


EXEMPLAR OF MORALLY PERFECTED WILL

























*JM, RH (II, 17-18)

Dante presents himself as a negative exemplar of morally perfected will. The extreme weakness of his fallen will is displayed in his inability to stay on the “straight way” of Virtue [diritta via: 3]; his recurrent vulnerability to the passions, especially “fear” [paura: 15, 19, 44, 53]; and his unbalanced manner of walking on the slope, with his “firm foot always the lower” [’l piè fermo sempre era ’l più basso: 30]. His reference to “the firm foot” harks back to the Augustinian allegory of the two “feet” of the soul: intellect and will. Dante’s intellect should be like the right foot moving forward, while his will should be like the left foot firmly planted, resistant to falling into sin. Dante’s near-suicidal state during the dark night of the smarrimento is the dangerous consequence of his weakness of will.

Dante presents Cato as a positive exemplar of morally perfected will. The extreme strength of his will as a Stoic sage was already legendary by the time Lucan portrayed him as a hero of republican virtues and an unflinching opponent of tyranny in the Pharsalia. The illumination of his face by the four stars, usually taken to signify the four Cardinal Virtues, suggests that his sternly unbending will was the blessed result of his perfect conformity to the moral life of active duty prescribed by Roman Stoicism. In sharp contrast to Dante, whose weak will brought him close to suicide in the dark wood, Cato famously committed suicide as an ultimate act of free will to express his principled stand against tyranny.


EXEMPLAR OF MARITAL FIDELITY


























*JM

A creature of insatiably “greedy appetite” [bramosa voglia: 98], the promiscuous she-wolf serves as a negative exemplar of marital fidelity. “Many are the creatures that she mates with,” as Virgil bluntly puts it [Molti son li animali a cui s’ammoglia: 100]. Nisus and Euryalus, the famous Virgilian couple whose loyalty to each other was heroically celebrated in the Aeneid, are literally split up in Virgil’s recollection of their fatal wounding [morì... Eurialo e Turno e Niso di ferute: 107-108].

A virtuous Roman abiding in Virgil’s circle of Hell, Cato’s wife Marcia serves as a positive exemplar of marital fidelity. Though divorced with her father’s and her husband’s approval so that she could be remarried to Cato’s friend Hortensius, Marcia is flatteringly portrayed by Virgil as a loyal wife to Cato. She implores Cato with her “chaste eyes” [occhi casti: 78] to continue thinking of her as his wife. Unmoved by this somewhat dubious portrayal, Cato re-divorces himself from her on Christian grounds: she is a damned soul now, while he is a harrowed saint. Cato’s loyalty is to God alone. His unyielding will is moved only by Virgil’s reference to Beatrice, the “lady from heaven” [donna del ciel: 91], whose loyalty to the unfaithful Dante is evidence of the supreme influence of divine love on the Blessed.


REBELLING AGAINST DIVINE LAW

[LEGGE]









*JM

Virgil, who was a perfectly law-abiding subject of the “good” Emperor Augustus, describes himself to Dante as a “rebel” to the Divine Emperor’s “law” [ribellante a la sua legge: 125].

Cato, who was a rebel against a would-be Roman Emperor’s tyrannical regime, pointedly asks Dante and Virgil whether their presence in Purgatory is a sign that “the laws of the abyss have thus been broken” [le leggi d’abisso così rotte: 46]. Ironically, by escaping the Inferno, Dante and Virgil have acted in perfect compliance with the Divine Will.


GUIDE PRESCRIBES NEW PATH: FIRST DOWN, THEN UP



*DM (II, 33), JM

Virgil directs Dante to go with him first down through Hell and then up to the summit of Purgatory to meet “a soul more fit to lead than I” [anima fia a ciò più di me degna:122].

Cato directs Dante to go with Virgil first down to the shore and then up another way to the summit of Purgatory [Poscia non sia di qua vostra reddita: 106].


FLATTERY
















*RM

Virgil is fulsomely praised by Dante as “the fountain that pours forth so broad a stream of speech” [quella fonte / che spandi di parlar sì largo fiume: 79-80] and then hailed as “the honour and light of all other poets” [O de li altri poeti onore e lume: 82]. Virgil seems to accept these praises as his due, for he does not rebuke Dante for his fan-worship.

Cato is flatteringly portrayed by Virgil as a republican hero who gave up his life for “liberty, which is so dear” [libertà...ch’è sì cara: 71-72] and as a faithful husband whose wife still loves him in the afterlife [78-80]. With a touch of envy, perhaps, Virgil even hails Cato as “O holy breast” [o santo petto: 80], though his own breast is far from holy and must endure the psychological torments of the First Circle. Cato is quick to rebuke Virgil for trying to capture his goodwill through “flatteries” [lusinghe: 92].


VIRGIL AS LIGHT







*JM

When Dante hails Virgil as the “light” [lume: 82] of all other poets, the image primarily refers to the glory of his worldly fame among ancient authors.

When Cato asks Dante “Who has guided you and who has been your lantern?” [Chi v’ha guidati, o che vi fu lucerna: 43], Virgil is again cast in the metaphoric role of a light. Here the image primarily refers to his moral role as a prophetic guide.


UNEXPECTED PAGAN AUTHORITY














*JM

As a pagan rhetorical “master” and “author” [maestro, autore: 85], Virgil ought to be stuck in Limbo with his coterie of famous classical poets. Released from the Inferno by the Divine Will, he has been endowed with new authority by Beatrice, whom he periphrastically identifies as a blessed soul “more worthy” than he [più di me degna: 122] to guide the pilgrim. His authority as a guide (especially in Purgatory) is wholly unexpected.

As a Stoic philosopher and celebrated political orator, Cato ought to abide in Limbo with the other noble pagans. Or, as a suicide, shouldn’t he be rooted in the wood of the Harpies? Released from the Inferno at the Harrowing, he has been endowed by God with new authority over the entire island of Purgatory – not just the shore [82]. His authority as a purgatorial guardian and guide is wholly unexpected.


ATTITUDE OF HUMBLE REVERENCE





*JM

In Virgil’s presence, Dante speaks with his “head lowered in shame” [con vergognosa fronte: 81].

In Cato’s presence, Virgil commands Dante with words and hand-gestures to show reverence with knee and brow [con parole e con mani e con cenni / reverenti mi fé le gambe e ’l ciglio: 50-51].


HUMILITY ON LOW LAND
























*JM

Dante is striving to reach the heights when he encounters the beasts. Of the three, the lion with “his head held high” [con la test’ alta: 47] is the beast most closely associated with Pride. Is the lion, as a negative exemplar of humility, a dream-like projection of Dante’s own besetting sin? Virgil compels Dante to consider another symbol of Pride in his allusion to the burning of “proud Ilium” [superbo Ilïón: 75]. This fallen site is heroically linked and morally contrasted to that “low-lying Italy” [quella umile Italia: 106] which the Veltro will eventually save despite its violent history. Here the literal meaning of the Italian adjective “umile” is close to the etymological meaning of its Latin ancestor “humilis, ” i.e. “on the ground,” hence “lowly.”

That humility will be a major moral theme throughout Purgatorio – and not just in its First Cornice where the Angel of Humility will erase the first P (for the peccatum of Pride) from Dante’s forehead – is emblematically revealed in the moral prominence of the reed, “the humble plant” [l’umile pianta: 135] that miraculously springs up in the soft mud of the Island “at its lowest point” [ad imo ad imo: 100]. As a proud soul, Dante must lower himself to the ground – literally enact “humility” in response to Virgil’s command that he kneel in reverence to Cato – if he is ever to be saved himself or morally positioned to save Italy through his poem. He remains on his knee for twenty tercets [49-108] until Cato vanishes from the scene.


GIRT / UNGIRT / REGIRT

















*JM

The leopard, “that beast with gaudy fur” [quella fiera a la gaetta pelle: 42], will be recollected by Dante on the brink of Malebolge when Virgil commands him to undo the cord from around his waist and hurl it into the abyss as a lure for another gaudy beast, Geryon. “I had a cord around my waist, and with it I once thought to take the leopard with the painted pelt” [Io avea una corda intorno cinta / e con essa pensai alcuna volta / prender la lonza a la pelle dipinta: Inf. 16.106-108], Dante recalls, filling in a detail not mentioned in Inferno 1.

Having remained ungirt since the Geryon episode, Dante will be regirt with a reed on the shore of Purgatory in fulfilment of Cato’s command to Virgil: “make sure you gird him with a straight reed” [fa che tu costui ricinghe / d’un giunco schietto: 94-95]. The straight reed contrasts with the pilgrim’s original cord, which was fittingly “coiled and knotted” [aggroppata e ravvolta: Inf. 16.111] as a lure for the Beast of Fraud.
